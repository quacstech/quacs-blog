We do Scrum. We have dailies.

A case study in teams that adopted Scrum by scheduling meetings — and quietly dropped the one that actually makes it work.

scrumagileretrospectiveprocessescase study

Every team does Scrum. Just ask them.

They have dailies. They have a board with columns. Someone, at some point, read the Scrum Guide or at least the first three pages of it. There are sprints, there are planning sessions, there are backlogs with varying degrees of grooming. It’s Scrum. Definitely Scrum.

There is, however, one event that keeps disappearing. Not dramatically — no one cancels it in anger. It just… slips. There’s a release. Someone is on leave. The sprint went long. Before you know it, the last retrospective was in Q3 and it is now Q1 of the following year.

The retrospective. The one meeting Scrum actually built the whole thing around.

What teams think Scrum is

In practice, most teams define their Scrum implementation by three things:

The daily standup — fifteen minutes, three questions, often fifteen minutes over time. This one never gets cancelled. Rain, bank holidays, production incidents — the daily happens. It is the one sacred ritual.

Sprint planning — a meeting that ranges from genuinely useful to a negotiation session where the team and the product owner politely disagree about velocity for ninety minutes and then commit to the same number they always commit to.

The board — columns named To Do, In Progress, In Review, Done. Sometimes there is a fifth column called Blocked that everyone pretends not to see.

This is not Scrum. This is Scrum-shaped. It has the silhouette of the framework without the mechanism that makes the framework self-correcting.

What the retrospective actually does

The retrospective is the only meeting in Scrum where the team looks at itself rather than at the work. Every other event is about the product. The retro is about the team and the process — how you work, not what you built.

Done properly, it produces one thing above all else: action points. Specific, assigned, time-boxed improvements to how the team operates. Not feelings. Not observations. Not a list of things that are bad. Actions, with owners and deadlines.

A retro without action points is a therapy session. Useful, perhaps, but not what it is for.

The case study

A product team of seven. Two-week sprints, solid velocity, decent quality. Dailies every morning, planning every other Monday. Retrospectives scheduled — and consistently moved, shortened, or replaced with a quick “anything to discuss?” at the end of planning.

The problems they had were not invisible. Context switching was causing quality issues. The definition of done was interpreted differently by different people. Deployments were manual and stressful. Everyone knew about these things. They came up in passing, in Slack, in one-to-ones.

They never became action points. They never had owners. They never had deadlines. So they stayed.

Eighteen months in, the same problems were still there — slightly worse, slightly more normalised. The team was not struggling. They were just not improving. Scrum without retrospectives is a framework that cannot learn.

What a working retrospective looks like

The format matters less than the discipline. Whether you use Start/Stop/Continue, the 4Ls, or just an open conversation — what matters is the output and what you do with it.

During the retro:

Gather observations first — what went well, what did not, what was confusing. Then identify the two or three things worth acting on. Not everything. Two or three.

For each action point, assign an owner (a person, not “the team”), agree a timeline, and if it requires actual work, create a ticket. Putting it in the backlog is not optional — if it does not exist as a ticket, it does not exist.

At the start of the next retro:

Before anything else, review the action points from the previous one. Were they done? If yes, close them and note the outcome. If not, why not — was it the wrong priority, the wrong owner, not enough time? Carry forward or close deliberately.

This single habit — opening every retro with a review of the last one — is what separates teams that improve from teams that talk about improving.

The uncomfortable truth

Skipping the retrospective is not a neutral decision. It is a decision to keep doing what you are doing, with the same friction, the same inefficiencies, and the same recurring problems — just without acknowledging it.

Teams that do this are not lazy or careless. They are usually busy and well-intentioned. But busy and well-intentioned is exactly the state the retrospective is designed to interrupt.

The daily tells you what happened today. Planning tells you what you will do next sprint. The retrospective is the only meeting that asks whether you are getting better at any of this.

It probably deserves to stay on the calendar.
